# Ancient Ciphers Hide Text, But Do They Destroy Structure?

This is Part 2 of the KL divergence experiment series. Part 1 showed that hashing a wordlist makes the byte distribution look much closer to uniform. This post asks a messier question: what happens when the transformation is an old or weak cipher instead of a hash?

The short answer is that hiding readability is not the same as destroying structure. Some classical ciphers change the symbols you see, while leaving the statistical shape almost untouched.

Repository / source / reference:

[`https://github.com/vishwaksen-1/words-kld-hash`](https://github.com/vishwaksen-1/words-kld-hash)

---

## What this post is about

This post compares plaintext against a small ladder of transformations:

- identity
- Caesar shift
- Vigenere
- columnar transposition
- substitution
- repeating-key XOR
- SHA-256 as a modern hash baseline

The focus is byte entropy and KL divergence to uniform. This post does not yet focus on n-grams or block repetition; those come later in the series.

---

## Background

Classical ciphers often make text unreadable to a human at first glance. But many of them preserve strong statistical clues.

A Caesar shift replaces each symbol with another symbol. A substitution cipher does the same with a larger permutation. Columnar transposition does not even replace symbols; it only reorders them. These transformations can hide the message while keeping byte counts exactly the same.

Vigenere and repeating-key XOR are different. They mix plaintext bytes with key bytes, so the output distribution can move closer to uniform. How much it moves depends heavily on the key.

---

## Why this matters

This is the gap between concealment and security.

If a cipher preserves enough statistical structure, an attacker may not need to read the text directly to learn something about it. Frequency analysis exists because natural language has strong patterns, and weak transformations often carry those patterns forward.

For this project, classical ciphers are useful because they create controlled failures. They help show what byte-level KL can catch and what it cannot.

---

## Constraints

- Cost: local-only experiment.
- Time: each transformation should run quickly across the wordlist.
- Hardware: no special hardware.
- Compute: byte-level statistics over `54368` plaintext bytes.
- Tools: Python implementations in `kldcrypto/ciphers.py`.
- Skills/context: educational cipher implementations, not production cryptography.
- Safety: do not imply that a low KL score makes a scheme safe.

The biggest constraint was keeping the comparison fair. Every method is measured with the same plaintext and the same byte-level metrics, even though the transformations have very different goals.

## System / Approach / Design

The experiment builds a transformation ladder:

```python
[
    ("Plain", "", plaintext),
    ("Identity", "", identity(plaintext)),
    ("Caesar", "shift=3", caesar_shift(plaintext, shift=3)),
    ("Vigenere", "key=lemon", vigenere_encrypt(plaintext, b"lemon")),
    ("Columnar", "cols=8", columnar_transposition(plaintext, columns=8)),
    ("Substitution", "sha-keyed", substitution_cipher(plaintext, key=b"classical-substitution")),
    ("XOR", "key=lemon", xor_repeating_key(plaintext, b"lemon")),
    ("SHA-256", "per word", sha256_each_word(words)),
]
```

I also ran key studies for Vigenere and XOR. The key lengths were:

```text
1, 2, 3, 5, 8, 13, 21, 34
```

For each length, the script tried known words, random words, random ASCII keys, and random byte keys.

Important decisions:

- Decision 1: keep Caesar, substitution, and columnar in the ladder.
- Why: they demonstrate transformations that preserve byte-frequency structure.
- Alternative: focus only on Vigenere and XOR.
- Tradeoff: the ladder is less modern, but the contrast is much clearer.

- Decision 2: test key randomness, not just key length.
- Why: a long human-word key is still not the same as random bytes.
- Alternative: vary only length.
- Tradeoff: more CSV rows, but a better explanation of the results.

## Tests / Observations

From `results/cipher_ladder.csv`:

| Method | Detail | Entropy bits/byte | KL(method \|\| uniform) |
| --- | --- | ---: | ---: |
| Plain | - | 4.2691 | 2.5860 |
| Caesar | shift=3 | 4.2691 | 2.5860 |
| Columnar | cols=8 | 4.2691 | 2.5860 |
| Substitution | sha-keyed | 4.2691 | 2.5860 |
| Vigenere | key=lemon | 4.8732 | 2.1674 |
| XOR | key=lemon | 4.7848 | 2.2286 |
| SHA-256 | per word | 7.9993 | 0.0005 |

Caesar, columnar transposition, and substitution all preserve the plaintext entropy and KL-to-uniform value. They change readability, but not the byte-frequency shape.

Vigenere with `key=lemon` moves the distribution a little closer to uniform. Repeating-key XOR with the same key does the same, but neither gets close to the SHA-256 baseline.

The key studies show the practical lesson: random byte keys flatten the distribution much more than human-word keys. For example, Vigenere with a 34-byte random byte key reached `KL(method || uniform) = 0.2117`, while a 34-byte known-word key stayed at `2.1899`.

---

## What failed / What was surprising

What broke:

- Direct `KL(Plain || Cipher)` can be misleading for relabeling ciphers.

Why it happened:

- Caesar and substitution move probability mass to different byte labels. The distribution shape is preserved, but the bins no longer line up.

How I diagnosed it:

- Caesar has the same entropy and same KL-to-uniform as plaintext, but direct KL between plaintext and Caesar becomes large.

What changed:

- I treated KL-to-uniform as the cleaner comparison for this post.

What tradeoff I accepted:

- This makes the post easier to interpret, while postponing order-sensitive analysis to the n-gram post.

---

## Result

- What works: byte KL clearly separates simple relabeling/reordering ciphers from hash-like output.
- What partially works: Vigenere and XOR improve as keys become longer and more random.
- What does not work yet: unigram byte KL cannot detect columnar transposition.
- What is untested: actual cryptanalysis, key recovery, and language-model-based attacks.

The main result is that weak ciphers may hide the text while preserving much of its statistical structure.

---

## What I learned

I thought the hard part would be seeing differences between the ciphers. It turned out the more interesting part was seeing which ciphers looked identical under this metric.

Columnar transposition is the cleanest warning sign. It changes the order of the message, but byte counts do not move at all. A unigram test has no reason to notice it.

We obviously see that SHA-256 is far closer to uniform. But the main lesson is that weak transformations can look different to a reader and identical to a byte counter.
